HIDEKI GARCIA GOO

M.A. Experimental Psychology, B.S. Electrical Engineering

I am a Master’s student in the Department of Psychology at the University of Texas Rio Grande Valley (UT-RGV). Since Fall 2018, I have been working with Dr. Megan Strait (initially as an undergraduate research assistant and now graduate research assistant) to study the reproduction and reinforcement of gendered and racialized marginalization via emergent forms of social interaction including social media, artificial intelligences, and humanlike robots.

I am interested in researching social bias, its consequences, and its manifestation across traditional forms of interaction (e.g., face-to-face) and technology-based interaction modalities that have emerged. I am especially interested in the transfer to and reproduction of bias in people’s interactions with agentic technologies (e.g., robots) and the capacity of such systems to address, deflect, and/or mitigate relational aggression in people’s interactions with other people.

EXPERIENCE

Social Systems Laboratory: I have assisted in the execution of six experiments - including the set up, IRB approval, participant recruitment/management, and data collection - on the associations between an agent’s human likeness and people’s aversion to it. I have also assisted in the analysis and publication of one of the experiments (Alvarez Perez, Garcia Goo, Sánchez Ramos, et al., 2020), as well as the analysis for and writing of a manuscript in-progress on the findings from a collection of four experiments demonstrating the reproducibility of the uncanny valley phenomenon across populations and with varying exposure to robotic technologies (submission anticipated in February, 2021).

Research Led: I have additionally independently proposed and led, with Dr. Strait’s mentorship, several extensions to her research. In the 2018-2019 academic year, I lead a team of three lab members in the execution, submission, and demonstration of a year-long design project involving construction and implementation of a novel robotic platform intended to promote human-robot cooperation through antisocial cues. The project was a finalist in the Student Design Competition of the 2019 International Conference on Human-Robot Interaction and invited for a live demonstration at the conference in Daegu, Korea. Furthermore, last year, I proposed another robotic platform to help users seeking to change speaking habits (i.e., using words that could be harmful to vulnerable populations) fulfill their goal. Again, this project is a finalist in the Student Design Competition 2021 which will take place this March.
